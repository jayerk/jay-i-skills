# Stakeholder Navigation & Influence

## Overview

This skill covers the human side of product leadership — understanding organizational dynamics, building alignment across competing interests, influencing without authority, and navigating complex decision-making environments. In a large regulated organization, the ability to navigate stakeholders is as important as the ability to build products. The right solution with the wrong stakeholder strategy fails.

Uses **Stakeholder Map**, **Engagement Sequence**, **Breadcrumb Trails**, and **Resistance Patterns** from `knowledge/vocabulary.md`. Understanding which mindsets a stakeholder holds (see `knowledge/behaviors-mindsets.md` → Leadership Mindsets) helps predict their reactions and tailor engagement strategy. Once the stakeholder landscape is mapped and the strategy is set, hand off to `executive-storytelling` to build the artifact that presents to the audience.

## When to Use This Skill

- Mapping stakeholders for a new initiative or program
- Building an influence strategy for a key decision
- Navigating competing priorities across organizational boundaries
- Preparing for a critical alignment meeting
- Managing resistance to a proposed change
- Building coalitions for transformation work

## When NOT to Use This Skill

- **Building the presentation** — if the stakeholder map exists and you're now creating the deck or memo, use `executive-storytelling`. This skill maps the audience; that skill builds the artifact.
- **Coaching and assessment** — if you're building a framework for evaluating teams or capabilities, use `coaching-frameworks`.
- **Writing requirements** — if the stakeholders are aligned and you're defining what to build, use `prd-writing`.

## Quality Bar

- **Stakeholder maps are honest** — they reflect actual power dynamics, not org chart authority. Influence, expertise, and gatekeeping roles are captured alongside formal authority.
- **Strategies are specific** — "Get buy-in from leadership" is not a strategy. "Schedule a 1:1 with VP of Risk to preview the compliance data before the steering committee" is.
- **Resistance is anticipated** — every influence strategy identifies likely objections and prepares responses. Surprise resistance means insufficient analysis.
- **Actions are sequenced** — the order of stakeholder engagement matters. Build base support before engaging decision-makers. Plant seeds before asking for the harvest.
- **Communication is tailored** — different stakeholders need different framings of the same initiative. What resonates with a CTO won't resonate with a Chief Risk Officer.

## Process

### Step 1: Map the Stakeholders

For each stakeholder relevant to the initiative:

| Dimension | Question |
|-----------|----------|
| **Authority** | Can they approve, block, or fund this? |
| **Influence** | Do others defer to their judgment? |
| **Interest** | How much do they care about this domain? |
| **Position** | Are they supportive, neutral, or resistant? |
| **Motivation** | What do they care about? What are they measured on? |
| **Communication style** | Data-driven? Narrative? Visual? Brief? |

### Step 2: Classify Stakeholder Roles

| Role | Description | Strategy |
|------|-------------|----------|
| **Champion** | Actively supports and advocates | Equip with talking points and data. Keep informed. |
| **Sponsor** | Has authority and is supportive | Provide decision-ready materials. Protect their credibility. |
| **Influencer** | Shapes opinions but may not decide | Engage early. Get input before the decision point. |
| **Gatekeeper** | Controls access or resources | Understand their criteria. Make compliance easy. |
| **Skeptic** | Has concerns but is persuadable | Address concerns directly. Show evidence. Invite to demos. |
| **Blocker** | Actively opposes | Understand their objection. Find common ground or route around. |

### Step 3: Build the Influence Strategy

**Sequencing matters.** The general pattern:

1. **Validate with experts** — get technical or domain credibility early
2. **Build with champions** — create momentum and social proof
3. **Preview with skeptics** — address concerns before the formal decision
4. **Engage gatekeepers** — ensure process requirements are met
5. **Present to decision-makers** — with support already built

For each key stakeholder, define:
- **What do they need to hear?** (Framed in their language and priorities)
- **What's the best format?** (1:1, small group, demo, memo, data)
- **When should they be engaged?** (Before/during/after the decision)
- **Who should engage them?** (You, your sponsor, a peer they respect)

### Step 4: Prepare for Resistance

Common resistance patterns and responses:

| Resistance | Underlying Concern | Response Strategy |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------------|
| "We've tried this before" | Risk aversion | Show what's different this time. Acknowledge past failures honestly. |
| "This isn't a priority" | Competing interests | Connect to their priorities. Show the cost of inaction. |
| "Who approved this?" | Authority concern | Ensure proper sponsorship is visible. Reference governance. |
| "Show me the data" | Credibility gap | Lead with evidence. Offer a pilot with measurable outcomes. |
| "This will break compliance" | Regulatory fear | Engage risk/compliance early. Show you've addressed their concerns. |

### Step 5: Execute and Adapt

- Track stakeholder positions over time — are they moving toward support?
- Adjust tactics based on feedback — if a framing isn't landing, reframe
- Build breadcrumb trails — small, low-risk exposures before the big ask
- Create win-win structures — "this helps you because..." not just "support this because it's right"
- Document commitments — after alignment conversations, send a brief summary of what was agreed

### Writing Quality Check

Before delivering final output, run a self-check against `.claude/skills/writing-triage.md`. Correct any flagged AI writing signals — structural repetition, vague generalities, voice uniformity, missing experiential detail. The output should read as practitioner-written, not machine-generated.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Org chart thinking** — assuming formal authority equals actual influence. Informal networks, expertise, and relationships often matter more.
- **Spray and pray** — presenting to everyone the same way and hoping someone supports it. Tailored engagement beats mass communication.
- **Ignoring resistance** — hoping blockers will come around on their own. They won't. Engage early or route around.
- **Burning sponsors** — over-using executive sponsors on low-stakes decisions. Save sponsor capital for decisions that truly need it.
- **Politics as a dirty word** — treating organizational navigation as beneath you. In a large organization, stakeholder management is a core product leadership skill, not a distraction from "real work."
