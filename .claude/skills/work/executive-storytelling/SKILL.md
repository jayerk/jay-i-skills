# Executive Storytelling

## Overview

This skill covers communication to executive audiences — presentations, strategic memos, status narratives, and persuasive documents. The core principle: executives don't need more information, they need better framing. Every executive artifact follows a narrative arc: situation, tension, resolution.

This is the **Depth to Brevity** skill — translating deep analysis into executive-ready language. Uses **BLUF** (Bottom Line Up Front) and **Narrative Arc** (Situation → Tension → Resolution) as core patterns. See `knowledge/vocabulary.md` for definitions.

## When to Use This Skill

- Building a presentation for senior leadership
- Writing a strategic memo or executive summary
- Framing a recommendation for approval
- Communicating program status in a way that drives action
- Translating technical or operational complexity into executive language
- Building a business case for investment

## When NOT to Use This Skill

- **Routine status updates** — a weekly status email doesn't need narrative arc engineering. Use `status-update` when available, or write it directly.
- **PRDs and requirements** — if the output is a requirements document, use `prd-writing`. Strategy informs the story, requirements are a different artifact.
- **Content writing** — blog posts, community content, and thought leadership have different audiences and formats. Use `community-content`.
- **Stakeholder influence work** — if the task is mapping stakeholders and building an engagement strategy, use `stakeholder-navigation`. This skill takes over when you're building the artifact to present.

## Quality Bar

- **Pyramid structure** — lead with the conclusion. The first slide or paragraph should contain the answer. Support follows.
- **One idea per slide/section** — cognitive load applies to executives too. Each unit of content makes one point.
- **Narrative arc** — every presentation tells a story: here's where we are (situation), here's the problem or opportunity (tension), here's what we should do (resolution).
- **Data, not adjectives** — "significant improvement" means nothing. "42% reduction in cycle time" means something. Quantify everything possible.
- **Action-oriented** — every executive document ends with a clear ask or decision point. What do you need from this audience?

## Process

### Step 1: Define the Communication Objective
- What decision do you need? (Approval, funding, alignment, awareness)
- Who is the audience? (C-suite, VP, cross-functional leaders)
- What do they already know? What do they care about?
- What's the one thing they should remember after this meeting?

If a stakeholder map exists (from `stakeholder-navigation`), use it here — audience positions, concerns, and motivations directly shape the narrative.

### Step 2: Build the Narrative Arc

**Situation → Tension → Resolution**

| Element | Purpose | Example |
|---------|---------|---------|
| **Situation** | Establish shared context | "Our product teams manage 340+ products across 12 domains" |
| **Tension** | Introduce the problem or opportunity | "But we have no consistent way to assess product health or prioritize investment" |
| **Resolution** | Present your recommendation | "We've built a Product Health Model that scores every product across 8 dimensions" |

### Step 3: Structure the Deck/Document

**For Presentations (8-12 slides max):**
1. Title + one-line framing
2. Executive summary (the entire story in one slide)
3. Situation (1-2 slides of context)
4. Tension (1-2 slides of the problem/opportunity)
5. Resolution (2-3 slides of the recommendation)
6. Evidence (1-2 slides of data, demos, or proof points)
7. Ask (1 slide with the specific decision or action needed)
8. Appendix (supporting detail for Q&A)

**For Memos (1-2 pages max):**
1. Bottom line up front (BLUF) — the recommendation in 2 sentences
2. Context — what the reader needs to know
3. Analysis — the logic chain supporting the recommendation
4. Recommendation — specific proposed action
5. Ask — what you need from the reader

### Step 4: Apply Visual Hierarchy
- **Harvey balls** for maturity or capability comparisons
- **Before/after** for transformation narratives
- **Trend lines** for progress over time
- **Scored matrices** for multi-dimensional assessments
- **Screenshots/demos** for "here's what it actually looks like" credibility

### Step 5: Stress-Test the Narrative
- Read only the slide titles in sequence — do they tell the complete story?
- Can the deck survive without a presenter? (It should.)
- Is the ask clear and specific? (Not "thoughts?" but "approve $X for Y")
- Would a skeptic find the logic chain compelling?
- Are you showing, not just telling? (Working demos > descriptions of demos)

## Output Formats

### Deck Outline
```
# [Presentation Title]
## Audience: [Who]
## Objective: [Decision needed]
## Date: [When]

---

### Slide 1: [Title]
**Key message:** [One sentence]
**Visual:** [Description of visual element]
**Speaker notes:** [What to say]

### Slide 2: [Title]
...
```

### Executive Memo
```
# [Subject Line]

**BLUF:** [Recommendation in 2 sentences]

## Context
[What the reader needs to know — 2-3 paragraphs max]

## Analysis
[Logic chain supporting the recommendation]

## Recommendation
[Specific proposed action with timeline and resources]

## Ask
[What you need from the reader — decision, approval, feedback]
```

## Anti-Patterns

- **Data dumps** — presenting all the data instead of the insight the data reveals. Executives need the "so what," not the raw material.
- **Burying the lead** — saving the recommendation for the last slide. Lead with it. Always.
- **Hedging** — "We might consider possibly exploring..." Take a position. You can acknowledge risks without undermining your own recommendation.
- **Missing the ask** — presenting without a clear decision point. If you don't need anything, it's a status update, not a presentation.
- **Complexity theater** — using complexity to signal thoroughness. Simplicity is harder and more valuable.
