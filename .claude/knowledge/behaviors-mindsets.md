# Behaviors & Mindsets: Teams and Leadership

What high-performing product teams and their leaders actually do (behaviors)
and believe (mindsets). Observable, not aspirational — if you can't see it,
it's not a behavior.

Used by `skills/coaching-frameworks/SKILL.md` for assessment design and by
`skills/gap-analysis/SKILL.md` for current-vs-target state evaluation. These
are the dimensions that matter when evaluating team and leadership health.

---

## Team Behaviors

Observable actions that high-performing product teams exhibit consistently.

### Discovery & Customer Centricity
- **Talk to users regularly** — at least weekly, not just during "discovery sprints." Conversations produce behavioral evidence, not just opinions.
- **Test assumptions before building** — low-confidence, high-impact assumptions get experiments, not features. The Opportunity Solution Tree is a living artifact, not a planning ceremony artifact.
- **Use data and research together** — quantitative signals (usage, funnels, support tickets) inform what to investigate; qualitative research (interviews, observation) explains why.
- **Challenge their own ideas** — actively seek disconfirming evidence. If nobody on the team disagrees with the direction, they haven't looked hard enough.

### Delivery & Execution
- **Ship small, learn fast** — work moves in increments that produce usable output, not in large batches that delay feedback.
- **Make trade-offs explicit** — when cutting scope or accepting risk, the team documents what was traded and why, not just what was shipped.
- **Own quality end-to-end** — the team doesn't throw work over the wall. They own discovery through delivery through verification.
- **Manage WIP actively** — limit work in progress. Finishing matters more than starting. A team that starts everything finishes nothing.

### Collaboration & Communication
- **Surface blockers early** — problems are raised when they're small, not when they're on fire. Psychological safety enables this; blame culture prevents it.
- **Align on outcomes, not tasks** — the team understands *why* they're doing the work, not just *what* they're building. Sprint goals connect to strategic themes.
- **Work across functions** — product, engineering, design, and domain experts collaborate throughout, not in sequential handoffs.
- **Share context generously** — decisions, rationale, and learnings are documented and accessible. New team members can get up to speed from artifacts, not just oral history.

### Continuous Improvement
- **Retrospect honestly** — retrospectives surface real problems with real accountability, not theater. "What would we do differently?" has specific answers.
- **Act on retro items** — improvement actions have owners and due dates. If the same issue appears in three retros, the team has a systemic problem, not an action item problem.
- **Measure what matters** — the team tracks leading indicators (discovery velocity, cycle time, WIP) not just lagging indicators (revenue, adoption).
- **Invest in enabling work** — the team allocates time for technical debt, tooling, and process improvement, not just feature delivery.

## Team Mindsets

Beliefs that drive the behaviors above. When a team struggles with a behavior,
look for the missing mindset.

| Mindset | What It Sounds Like | Opposite (Anti-Pattern) |
|---------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| **Outcome ownership** | "We own the problem, not just the feature." | "We built what was asked for. Not our fault it didn't work." |
| **Intellectual humility** | "We might be wrong. Let's test it." | "We've been doing this for years. We know what users need." |
| **Customer empathy** | "What does this feel like from their side?" | "Users will figure it out." |
| **Sustainable pace** | "We can't sprint forever. Let's pace ourselves." | "We'll catch up on debt next quarter." (They never do.) |
| **Transparency** | "Let's share what we know, including the bad news." | "Let's not raise this until we have a solution." |
| **Systems thinking** | "If we change this, what else does it affect?" | "That's another team's problem." |
| **Learning orientation** | "What did we learn from that failure?" | "Who's responsible for that failure?" |
| **Craft pride** | "Internal tools deserve the same care as external products." | "It's just an internal tool. Good enough is good enough." |

## Leadership Behaviors

Observable actions that effective product leaders exhibit. "Leadership" here
means anyone who shapes direction, coaches teams, or makes investment
decisions — not just people with "leader" in their title.

### Strategic Clarity
- **Set direction, not directives** — provide the strategic context (vision, constraints, success criteria) and trust teams to find the solution. Empowered teams need context, not prescriptions.
- **Say no clearly** — guardrails are explicit. What we're *not* doing is as important as what we are. If everything is a priority, nothing is.
- **Connect work to outcomes** — every initiative links to a strategic theme. If a team can't articulate why they're building something, leadership hasn't provided enough context.
- **Revisit strategy regularly** — strategy is a living hypothesis, not an annual decree. Quarterly check: is the crux still the crux?

### Enablement & Coaching
- **Build capability, not dependency** — the goal of coaching is to make yourself unnecessary. If the team can't function without you, you're a bottleneck, not a leader.
- **Make the right thing the easy thing** — build tools, frameworks, and processes that make good practice the default behavior. Don't publish guidelines and hope for adoption.
- **Coach with questions, not answers** — "What options did you consider?" teaches more than "Here's what you should do."
- **Meet teams where they are** — a team at maturity level 2 needs different coaching than a team at level 4. Don't prescribe level-5 practices to a team that hasn't mastered level 3.

### Investment & Prioritization
- **Allocate by lifecycle stage** — a Growth-stage product gets different R/I/T allocation than a Maturity-stage product. One size does not fit all.
- **Use frameworks, not opinions** — prioritization is structured (Balanced Breakthrough, weighted scoring, D/F/V) not political (HiPPO, loudest voice).
- **Fund teams, not projects** — stable teams with clear ownership outperform project-based staffing. Team Topologies is an investment decision, not an org chart exercise.
- **Kill what isn't working** — kill criteria exist and are enforced. Zombie initiatives consume resources without delivering value.

### Transparency & Communication
- **Communicate the why** — every decision includes rationale. Teams can disagree with the decision, but they should never have to guess why it was made.
- **Share bad news fast** — escalate problems when they're small. A leader who hides problems models hiding problems.
- **Translate depth to brevity** — compress deep analysis into executive-ready language. The depth informs the brevity. A VP should be able to act on the summary in 30 seconds.
- **Build alignment before asking for approval** — breadcrumb trails, coalition building, previews with skeptics. The formal ask should never be the first time someone hears the idea.

### Organizational Health
- **Protect cognitive load** — actively manage how much context teams must hold. When cognitive load exceeds capacity, quality drops and delivery slows.
- **Model the behavior** — if you want teams to retrospect honestly, retrospect honestly yourself. If you want teams to use data, use data yourself.
- **Create psychological safety** — teams that fear blame don't surface problems, don't take risks, and don't innovate. Safety is the foundation of everything else.
- **Invest in the system, not just the output** — transformation enablement means building infrastructure (tools, frameworks, training) that changes how the organization works, not just what it produces.

## Leadership Mindsets

Beliefs that drive the leadership behaviors above.

| Mindset | What It Sounds Like | Opposite (Anti-Pattern) |
|---------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| **Multiplier orientation** | "My job is to make everyone else more effective." | "My job is to make the right decisions." |
| **Servant leadership** | "What do my teams need from me?" | "What do I need my teams to do?" |
| **Long-term thinking** | "What will this decision look like in 2 years?" | "What will this decision look like in this quarter's metrics?" |
| **Institutional courage** | "This is the right thing even if it's uncomfortable." | "Let's not rock the boat right now." |
| **Gearing ratio awareness** | "Where can small effort create large impact?" | "Let's throw more people at it." |
| **Ecosystem thinking** | "How does this affect adjacent teams and products?" | "That's outside my scope." |
| **Evidence over intuition** | "What does the data say? What do users do?" | "In my experience, this is what works." |
| **Enablement over control** | "How do I build the environment for good decisions?" | "How do I make sure the right decisions get made?" |

## How to Use This Reference

**For coaching-frameworks:** These behaviors and mindsets become assessment
dimensions. Each behavior can be scored on a maturity scale (Emerging → Leading)
using the pattern in `skills/coaching-frameworks/SKILL.md`.

**For gap-analysis:** Compare a team's current behaviors against these reference
behaviors. The gaps identify coaching priorities. Use `skills/gap-analysis/SKILL.md`.

**For stakeholder-navigation:** Understanding which mindsets a stakeholder holds
helps predict their reactions and tailor engagement strategy.

**For hiring and development:** These behaviors define "what good looks like"
for product team members and leaders. They can inform interview rubrics,
development plans, and performance conversations.
