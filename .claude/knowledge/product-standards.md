# Product Standards: 9 Dimensions Across 3 Pillars

A reference standard for what "good" looks like in product management practice.
10 sub-practices organized into 3 pillars. Each sub-practice is documented across
9 structural dimensions — 4 defining the standard, 4 defining how to implement it,
plus a cross-cutting dependency map.

Designed to power self-assessment, coaching conversations, and gap analysis — not
to be a compliance checklist.

---

## Product Definition

A product must meet the following criteria:

- The customer (whether external or internal) and their needs can be clearly identified
- It creates measurable economic value
- Has an enduring lifecycle

*A customer is the primary beneficiary, whether internal or external, of the product's
value proposition — not just any stakeholder.*

---

## The 9 Structural Dimensions

Every sub-practice is documented across these 9 dimensions, split into two layers.

### Standard Layer — the "what"

| Dimension | What It Defines |
|-----------|----------------|
| **1. Purpose & Value** | Why this sub-practice matters |
| **2. Principles** | Guidelines to drive consistency and lead to better outcomes |
| **3. Success Criteria** | What good (& bad) looks like, incl. examples, checklists, and 5-level maturity rubric (harvey balls) |
| **4. Governance** | Who owns the standard and how it will be maintained |

### How-To Layer — the "how"

| Dimension | What It Defines | Jay's Refinement |
|-----------|----------------|-----------------|
| **5. Execution Guidance** | How to implement the practice | Minimum viable how-to with pointers to frameworks — not an implementation manual. Keep thin so teams adapt to context. |
| **6. Applicability** | How this practice differs across contexts | Organized by lifecycle stage (Concept/Launch/Growth/Maturity/Sunset) AND product type (customer-facing, internal platform, regulatory) so teams can quickly find their row. |
| **7. Measurement** | How adoption and effectiveness are tracked | Include leading indicators (are teams doing the practice?) alongside lagging indicators (is it producing outcomes?). Without leading indicators you only find out 6 months later that adoption stalled. |
| **8. Adoption Path** | How teams get started and reach maturity | What getting started looks like → what good looks like at 6 months → what embedded looks like. Pragmatic on-ramp, not a heavy change management plan. |

### Cross-Cutting

| Dimension | What It Defines |
|-----------|----------------|
| **9. Dependencies** | Which other standards are prerequisites or companions. Prevents cherry-picking that produces incoherent adoption. |

## The 3 Pillars and 10 Sub-Practices

### Pillar 1: Customer-Centered Product Design

Practices to understand and address the needs, behaviors, and experiences of
customers and employees throughout the product lifecycle.

#### 1. Customer & Market Insights

- Research insights analysis & observability
- Proactive monitoring of customer & employee signals
- Iterative prototyping
- Usability testing & feedback

##### Maturity Rubric

| Level | Harvey Ball | Observable Behaviors | Evidence You'd See |
|-------|-----------|---------------------|-------------------|
| 1 — Emerging | ○ | No systematic research. Decisions based on assumptions or loudest stakeholder voice. No feedback loops after launch. | No research repository. PRDs cite no user evidence. Post-launch surveys absent. |
| 2 — Developing | ◔ | Some research happens but ad-hoc — triggered by executive request or crisis. Insights aren't centralized. Teams do research when asked but don't seek it out. | Scattered interview notes. Research done for specific projects but not reusable. Usability testing happens occasionally. |
| 3 — Established | ◑ | Regular discovery cadence (e.g., weekly interviews). Research repository exists and is referenced in decisions. Usability testing before launch is standard. Prototypes tested with users before build commitment. | Discovery backlog maintained. Interview insights tagged and searchable. Prototype test results documented. Product decisions cite specific research. |
| 4 — Advanced | ◕ | Continuous signal monitoring across channels (support, analytics, research, sales). Research methods matched to question type. Prototype-test cycles are standard practice. Insights quantified and tracked over time. | Signal dashboard reviewed monthly. Method selection documented (why interviews vs. observation vs. data). Research quality improving quarter-over-quarter. |
| 5 — Leading | ● | Organization-wide evidence culture. Discovery feeds directly into opportunity solution trees. Research informs not just features but strategy and portfolio decisions. Team teaches discovery practices to others. | Research referenced in strategy documents. OST maintained and current. Other teams request discovery coaching. Research methods evolving. |

#### 2. Segmentation & Personas

- Enduring customer profiles reflecting behavioral, needs, or value-based segments
- Consistent and proactive personalized engagement across channels

##### Maturity Rubric

| Level | Harvey Ball | Observable Behaviors | Evidence You'd See |
|-------|-----------|---------------------|-------------------|
| 1 — Emerging | ○ | No defined segments. Product treats all users as one homogeneous group. No personas. Personalization absent. | PRDs reference "the user" generically. No segment definitions in any artifact. |
| 2 — Developing | ◔ | Some informal segments exist but based on demographics or org chart, not behaviors/needs. Personas created once for a project but not maintained or referenced. | Stale persona documents. Segments used in marketing but not product decisions. Teams can't name their segments without looking them up. |
| 3 — Established | ◑ | Behavioral or needs-based segments defined and actively used. Personas updated at least annually. Product decisions reference specific segments. Engagement approach varies by segment. | Segment definitions in product strategy docs. Personas accessible to full team. Feature prioritization considers segment impact. |
| 4 — Advanced | ◕ | Segment performance tracked (adoption, satisfaction, retention by segment). Personalization informed by segment data. Cross-functional teams share and co-own the segment model. | Segment dashboards. A/B testing by segment. Design, product, and marketing align on segment definitions. |
| 5 — Leading | ● | Segments evolve with market signals. Predictive modeling informs segment strategy. Engagement personalized at scale. New segments identified proactively, not reactively. | Segment model versioned and change-logged. Emerging segments detected through data. Other teams adopt the segmentation approach. |

#### 3. Customer & Employee Journeys

- Continuously map and refine enduring customer journeys to align experiences across the business
- Maintain a centralized, accessible design system for consistent, human-centered experience

##### Maturity Rubric

| Level | Harvey Ball | Observable Behaviors | Evidence You'd See |
|-------|-----------|---------------------|-------------------|
| 1 — Emerging | ○ | No journey maps. Experience understood feature-by-feature, not end-to-end. No design system. Each team builds UI independently. | No journey artifacts. Inconsistent UX patterns across features. Customer handoffs between teams are friction points no one owns. |
| 2 — Developing | ◔ | Some journey maps created for specific projects but static — not maintained after the project ends. Design system started but adoption inconsistent. | Project-specific journey maps gathering dust. Partial component library. Teams aware of journeys but don't reference them in planning. |
| 3 — Established | ◑ | Enduring journey maps maintained and updated. Pain points identified, prioritized, and tracked. Design system in place with clear ownership and adoption across teams. | Journey maps reviewed quarterly. Pain points in the backlog with journey context. Design system usage metrics tracked. |
| 4 — Advanced | ◕ | Journey maps connected to metrics (time-on-task, error rates, satisfaction per stage). Friction points tracked quantitatively. Cross-team alignment on journey ownership. Design system evolving based on usage data. | Journey-stage dashboards. Friction reduction visible in metrics over time. Design system contributions from multiple teams. |
| 5 — Leading | ● | Journey-driven strategy — product decisions start from the journey, not from feature requests. Operations optimize around customer journey stages. Design system is a competitive advantage. | Journey maps in strategy presentations. Org structure reflects journey stages. Design system adopted beyond the original product. |

### Pillar 2: Measurable Economic Value

Practices to enable data-informed teams to observe and optimize the impact of a product.

#### 4. Product Economic Insights

- Observability of key economic KPI driving the primary source of value of the product
- Leading indicators of the product's performance

##### Maturity Rubric

| Level | Harvey Ball | Observable Behaviors | Evidence You'd See |
|-------|-----------|---------------------|-------------------|
| 1 — Emerging | ○ | No economic model. Value discussed anecdotally ("users like it"). Metrics limited to vanity counts (page views, logins). No connection between product activity and business outcomes. | No unit economics documented. Business cases use gut estimates. Finance and product don't share a common language. |
| 2 — Developing | ◔ | Basic metrics tracked (revenue, active users) but not connected to value drivers. No leading indicators — team only knows something went wrong after the quarterly review. Cost of the product understood at budget level, not unit level. | Dashboard exists but rarely reviewed. Metrics not referenced in prioritization. No one can state the north star metric. |
| 3 — Established | ◑ | Key economic KPIs identified, monitored, and owned. Unit economics understood (cost to serve, value per transaction/user). Leading indicators tracked alongside lagging. North star metric defined and shared. | Economic model documented. Monthly metric reviews. Leading indicators (e.g., activation rate, time-to-value) on the dashboard next to lagging ones. |
| 4 — Advanced | ◕ | Economic model informs decisions — prioritization references economic impact. Growth and retention economics modeled. Cost of delay quantified for backlog items. Finance and product co-own the model. | Cost of delay in prioritization artifacts. Retention cohort analysis. Economic impact column in scoring matrices. Model updated quarterly. |
| 5 — Leading | ● | Full economic observability. Growth loops mapped and optimized. Economic insights drive portfolio allocation, not just feature decisions. Model stress-tested against scenarios. | Growth loop diagrams in strategy docs. Portfolio allocation justified by economic model. Other product groups adopt the economic framework. |

#### 5. Value-Based Sequencing

- Prioritization using value, risk, and effort scoring
- Work breakdown practices with understanding of readiness to support flow
- Documented trade-offs and rationale for sequencing decisions

##### Maturity Rubric

| Level | Harvey Ball | Observable Behaviors | Evidence You'd See |
|-------|-----------|---------------------|-------------------|
| 1 — Emerging | ○ | Everything is P1. Sequencing driven by stakeholder volume, executive recency bias, or whoever is loudest. No scoring framework. Trade-offs undocumented. | No prioritization criteria. Backlog is an unsorted wish list. "Why are we doing this first?" has no defensible answer. |
| 2 — Developing | ◔ | Some prioritization criteria exist but applied inconsistently. No value hypothesis per item. Work breakdown happens but without readiness checks — items get sequenced that the team can't execute. | Criteria documented somewhere but not referenced in planning. Priority assigned by gut. Blocked items discovered mid-sprint. |
| 3 — Established | ◑ | Desirability/Feasibility/Viability scoring applied consistently. Value hypotheses documented per item. Force-ranked backlog (no ties). Flow readiness checked before commitment. Trade-offs documented. | Scoring matrix in use. Value hypothesis column populated. Backlog has a clear sequence. Readiness checklist (WIP, dependencies, skills) applied. |
| 4 — Advanced | ◕ | Closed-loop: value realization tracked against hypotheses. Scoring calibrated quarterly (did we over/underestimate?). Cost of delay informs sequence. Trade-off rationale reviewed and refined. | Quarterly value hypothesis review. Calibration adjustments documented. Cost of delay column in scoring. Stakeholders reference the scoring in discussions. |
| 5 — Leading | ● | Portfolio-level sequencing optimized across products. Cost of delay is the primary sequencing driver. Teams self-manage sequencing within guardrails. Prioritization practices taught to other teams. | Portfolio-level scoring. Teams adjust sequence without escalation. Other teams request prioritization coaching. Sequencing withstands executive challenge. |

#### 6. Value Guardrails & Realization

- Guardrails for customer experience, reliability, and strategic capacity allocation
- Clear stop/pivot/kill criteria and cost allocation across run/grow/transform
- Benefits forecasting and value hypothesis standards

##### Maturity Rubric

| Level | Harvey Ball | Observable Behaviors | Evidence You'd See |
|-------|-----------|---------------------|-------------------|
| 1 — Emerging | ○ | No guardrails. No kill criteria. Initiatives run until budget is exhausted or a leader loses interest. No benefits tracking. Capacity allocation is whatever got funded. | Zombie initiatives consuming resources. No post-launch value check. R/I/T allocation unknown or accidental. |
| 2 — Developing | ◔ | Some guardrails exist (e.g., budget caps) but not tied to value outcomes. Kill criteria discussed in planning but not enforced — nothing actually gets killed. Benefits forecasted in business cases but never revisited. | Business cases with benefits that no one checks. "We should kill that" said but not acted on. Capacity allocation stated but not measured. |
| 3 — Established | ◑ | CX, reliability, and capacity guardrails defined and monitored. R/I/T allocation targets set by lifecycle stage. Kill criteria enforced — initiatives actually stopped when triggers hit. Value hypotheses tracked post-launch. | Guardrail metrics on dashboard. R/I/T actuals reviewed quarterly. At least one initiative killed based on criteria. Benefits realization report exists. |
| 4 — Advanced | ◕ | Guardrails self-monitored by teams (not just leadership). Benefits realization reviewed quarterly and fed back into planning. R/I/T allocation adjusted based on evidence. Kill decisions made proactively, not as a last resort. | Teams flag their own guardrail breaches. Allocation shifts documented with rationale. Benefits realization trends improving. Kill criteria trigger automated alerts. |
| 5 — Leading | ● | Self-governing teams operate within guardrails without escalation. Guardrails embedded in the way of working, not bolted on. Value realization is a cultural norm — teams expect to prove value. R/I/T allocation is a strategic lever teams understand and use. | No escalation needed for routine guardrail decisions. New teams adopt guardrails naturally. Value realization feeds strategy. Balanced Breakthrough allocation debated at team level. |

### Pillar 3: Enduring Lifecycle

Practices to enable product predictability, velocity, and adaptability from
concept to sunset.

#### 7. Measurable Outcomes

- Vision for the value that a product will deliver to a customer
- KPIs for ongoing performance monitoring

##### Maturity Rubric

| Level | Harvey Ball | Observable Behaviors | Evidence You'd See |
|-------|-----------|---------------------|-------------------|
| 1 — Emerging | ○ | No stated vision or outcome targets. Success = "we shipped it." KPIs absent or limited to output metrics (features delivered, story points completed). | No product vision document. No OKRs or outcome targets. Retrospectives discuss what was built, not what it achieved. |
| 2 — Developing | ◔ | Vision exists but vague or aspirational without measurable targets. KPIs defined but not monitored regularly. Team tracks outputs (velocity, throughput), not outcomes (adoption, satisfaction, value). | Vision statement that can't be falsified. KPI dashboard that nobody reviews. Quarterly reviews focus on delivery, not impact. |
| 3 — Established | ◑ | Clear vision tied to specific customer value. KPIs monitored regularly with defined targets. Outcome targets inform planning — what we build connects to what we expect to achieve. Leading and lagging indicators both present. | Vision statement with measurable outcomes. Monthly KPI reviews with actions. Planning references target outcomes. Leading indicators (activation, engagement) tracked alongside lagging (retention, revenue). |
| 4 — Advanced | ◕ | Outcome-based planning — teams start from "what outcome do we need to move?" not "what feature should we build?" Teams own their metrics and adjust approach when outcomes stall. KPIs cascade from strategy to team. | Outcome-first planning artifacts. Teams proactively flag stalled metrics. KPI hierarchy connecting product metrics to business outcomes. Mid-cycle adjustments based on outcome data. |
| 5 — Leading | ● | Outcome-driven culture. Vision revisited and adapted as market evolves. KPIs cascade from strategy to team to individual. Outcomes inform portfolio decisions, not just product decisions. | Vision updates logged with rationale. Individual goals tied to product outcomes. Portfolio reviews reference outcome trends. Other teams model their outcome practice on this one. |

#### 8. Strategy & Roadmap

- Roadmap artifact to bring vision to life over multi-year planning horizon
- Objectives & key results to align teams to the roadmap

##### Maturity Rubric

| Level | Harvey Ball | Observable Behaviors | Evidence You'd See |
|-------|-----------|---------------------|-------------------|
| 1 — Emerging | ○ | No roadmap, or a feature list with dates masquerading as one. No strategic context — teams know what to build but not why. No OKRs. | Gantt chart of features. No "why" behind any item. Teams can't explain how their work connects to business strategy. |
| 2 — Developing | ◔ | Roadmap exists but date-and-feature oriented. Loosely tied to strategy — connection is implied, not explicit. OKRs exist but are activity-based ("launch X") not outcome-based. Updated sporadically. | Roadmap as a project plan. OKRs that are really a task list. Roadmap updated when executives ask for it. Strategy document and roadmap are disconnected artifacts. |
| 3 — Established | ◑ | Outcome-based roadmap organized by time horizons (Now/Next/Later/Vision). OKRs align teams to strategic themes. Roadmap updated quarterly. Strategic context (guiding policy, themes, allocation) explicit. | Roadmap references strategic themes. OKRs with measurable key results. Quarterly roadmap review cadence. Teams can articulate how their work connects to strategy. |
| 4 — Advanced | ◕ | Strategy as living practice — roadmap connects to economic model and evolves with new information. OKRs reviewed and adjusted mid-cycle when data warrants. Roadmap withstands executive challenge because rationale is documented. | Mid-cycle OKR adjustments with rationale. Roadmap changes traced to new evidence. Strategy-to-roadmap-to-OKR traceability. Roadmap used in executive conversations, not created for them. |
| 5 — Leading | ● | Multi-year strategic vision drives roadmap. Teams contribute to strategy, not just execute it. Roadmap is a communication tool that builds organizational alignment. Strategic pivots are fast because the thinking infrastructure exists. | Teams propose strategic themes. Roadmap referenced across the organization. Strategy documents versioned with decision log. Other product groups model their strategy practice on this one. |

#### 9. Adaptive Delivery Plans

- Structured idea intake
- Refined backlog for predictable value delivery
- Proactive product risk management
- Delivery at a sustainable pace

##### Maturity Rubric

| Level | Harvey Ball | Observable Behaviors | Evidence You'd See |
|-------|-----------|---------------------|-------------------|
| 1 — Emerging | ○ | No delivery cadence. Work managed ad-hoc — items enter the backlog by interrupt. No intake process. Risks discovered at launch or after. Pace driven by urgency, not sustainability. | No backlog refinement. Items added mid-sprint without evaluation. No risk register. Team burnout or unpredictable delivery. |
| 2 — Developing | ◔ | Some structure but inconsistent. Backlog exists but not regularly refined — stale items accumulate. Risks tracked reactively (after they materialize). Intake exists but is informal. | Backlog with items older than 6 months untouched. Occasional refinement sessions. Risk discussions happen in retros after the fact. Intake is "send a Slack message." |
| 3 — Established | ◑ | Structured intake with evaluation criteria. Backlog refined regularly with clear priorities. Wave-based or sprint-based delivery with predictable cadence. Risk register maintained and reviewed at checkpoints. Sustainable pace monitored. | Intake form or process documented. Backlog groomed weekly/biweekly. Risk register updated at wave boundaries. Velocity stable quarter-over-quarter. Team capacity planned. |
| 4 — Advanced | ◕ | Delivery cadence adapted to lifecycle stage (tighter in Launch, broader in Maturity). Predictable velocity enables reliable forecasting. Risk management proactive — emerging risks identified before they hit. Intake connected to prioritization framework. | Lifecycle-aware cadence documented. Delivery forecasts within 15% accuracy. Risks mitigated before escalation. Intake feeds directly into D/F/V scoring. |
| 5 — Leading | ● | Lifecycle-adapted delivery is second nature. Teams self-organize within the framework. Continuous improvement on delivery practices — team experiments with process. Delivery practices shared as a model for other teams. | Teams adjust their own cadence. Process experiments logged. Other teams shadow delivery practices. Delivery and strategy are seamlessly connected. |

#### 10. Routines for Activation

- Routines that support refinement and adjustment of roadmaps, backlogs, and plans
- Strong communication and transparency across all stakeholders & teams
  - Cross-functional touchpoints
  - Launch enablement

##### Maturity Rubric

| Level | Harvey Ball | Observable Behaviors | Evidence You'd See |
|-------|-----------|---------------------|-------------------|
| 1 — Emerging | ○ | No regular routines. Alignment happens by accident or escalation. Launch is "push to prod and tell people later." Cross-functional coordination absent — teams discover conflicts after the fact. | No recurring meetings with agenda. Launch surprises stakeholders. Support, training, and ops learn about changes from customers. |
| 2 — Developing | ◔ | Some routines exist (e.g., weekly standup) but attendance or follow-through inconsistent. Launch communication is ad-hoc — some people get told, some don't. Cross-functional touchpoints happen but aren't structured. | Meetings without consistent agendas or outcomes. Launch emails sent to incomplete distribution lists. Cross-functional input requested late. |
| 3 — Established | ◑ | Regular routines with clear purpose: backlog refinement, roadmap review, stakeholder updates, retrospectives. Launch enablement checklist used — support, training, ops, compliance engaged before launch. Cross-functional touchpoints at key milestones. | Routine calendar with cadence and owners. Launch checklist with sign-offs. Stakeholder update artifacts. Cross-functional sync at wave transitions. |
| 4 — Advanced | ◕ | Routines drive cross-functional alignment proactively — issues surfaced in routines, not escalations. Communication is proactive and audience-tailored. Launch readiness tiered by risk (lightweight for low-risk, full enablement for high-risk). | Routine outcomes tracked (decisions made, actions completed). Launch tiers defined and applied. Stakeholder satisfaction with communication improving. Cross-functional teams co-own launch readiness. |
| 5 — Leading | ● | Self-governing cadences — teams evolve their routines based on what works, not mandates. Launch enablement is a competitive advantage — stakeholders trust the process. Communication creates organizational alignment beyond the immediate team. | Teams modify their own routine cadence with rationale. Launch post-mortems drive process improvement. Routines referenced as a model by other teams. Communication artifacts reused across the organization. |
