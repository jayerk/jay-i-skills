# Principles: How I Think About Product Work

## Core Beliefs

### 1. Products Are Systems, Not Feature Lists
A product is a system of interconnected capabilities, teams, users, and constraints. Changing one part affects others. Good product work starts by understanding the system, not by listing what to build.

**In practice:** Systems mapping is the first diagnostic move, not an optional step. Before scoping any initiative, identify the actors, dependencies, and feedback loops — grounded in customer and employee experiences, not abstract boxes on a diagram. Define boundaries explicitly: what's in scope, what's out of scope, and what would change the bounds. Being hyper-specific about what's out lets you test whether the system definition is good or needs revision.

Some system artifacts should be built once and iterated on — journeys, personas, service maps — because they compound in value over time. Others — solution designs, implementation plans — should be built fresh for each initiative, informed by those stable artifacts. Know which is which, and recognize that you may need to meet teams where they are rather than where the model says they should be.

### 2. Show, Don't Tell
A working demo communicates more effectively than any document. Visual artifacts — interactive prototypes, scored rubrics, health dashboards — create shared understanding faster than written arguments.

**In practice:** When presenting a concept, build the thing (or a working version of it) rather than describing what the thing would be. A self-running HTML demo is worth more than a deck explaining the concept.

### 3. Encode Expertise Into Reusable Systems
If you've solved a problem well, encode the solution so others can apply it. Scoring rubrics, assessment dimensions, maturity models, and decision frameworks turn individual expertise into organizational capability.

**In practice:** After solving a hard problem, ask: "Can I turn this into a framework others can use?" If yes, build the rubric, the scoring model, the assessment template. Make your thinking portable.

### 4. Iterate, Don't Perfect
Ship a working version and refine based on real usage. Waiting for perfect means waiting forever — and you learn more from a v1 in production than a v3 in your head.

**In practice:** Set a quality bar for "good enough to use," hit it, ship it, then iterate. The first version teaches you what the second version needs to be.

### 5. Craft Is Not Optional
Internal tools deserve the same care as external products. Sloppy frameworks get ignored. Ugly tools don't get adopted. Quality drives adoption, and adoption drives impact.

**In practice:** Every artifact should be well-structured, clearly written, and visually clean. This isn't vanity — it's how you get people to actually use what you build.

### 6. Teams Are the Product
The way teams are organized determines what they can build. Team Topologies isn't just theory — it's the operating system for how product work happens. Stream-aligned, platform, enabling, complicated subsystem — these aren't labels, they're design decisions.

**In practice:** When a product team is struggling, look at the team structure before looking at the backlog. Cognitive load, interaction modes, and team boundaries explain more than missing features.

### 7. Prioritization Is a First-Class Discipline
Most product teams have too many good ideas and not enough rigorous prioritization. Frameworks like Balanced Breakthrough, weighted scoring, and opportunity mapping turn prioritization from politics into analysis.

**In practice:** Never prioritize by loudest voice or highest-paid person's opinion. Use a structured framework, make the criteria explicit, and score transparently.

### 8. Context Over Control
Empowered teams need context, not directives. Product leaders provide the strategic context — the vision, the constraints, the success criteria — and trust teams to find the best solution.

**In practice:** As an enablement leader, your job is to give teams the strategic context, the frameworks, and the assessment tools — then let them own the solution. Don't coach by prescribing answers; coach by sharpening the team's ability to find their own. If you're dictating solutions, you've become a bottleneck instead of a multiplier.

### 9. Discovery Never Stops
Product discovery isn't a phase — it's a continuous practice. Every week should include some amount of customer interaction, assumption testing, and opportunity mapping.

**In practice:** Embed discovery into the cadence, not the roadmap. Weekly customer touchpoints, regular assumption reviews, and a living Opportunity Solution Tree that connects outcomes to opportunities to solutions. If the tree isn't updated weekly, discovery has stopped — regardless of what the roadmap says.

Discovery is also how you pressure-test system boundaries. The stable artifacts from systems mapping — journeys, personas, service maps — stay current because discovery keeps validating them against real customer and employee experiences. When discovery reveals that a boundary no longer holds, that's a signal to revise the system map, not just the backlog.

### 10. Transformation Is Infrastructure Work
Changing how an organization does product work requires building infrastructure — tools, frameworks, training, assessment systems — not just publishing guidelines. You change behavior by changing the environment.

**In practice:** Don't write a "best practices" document and expect adoption. Build the tool that makes the best practice the default behavior. Make the right thing the easy thing.
